# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - RED Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Sebastian (Variation 5.2 - Detailed/Explanatory Style)
**Age:** 32
**Location:** Aarhus, Brabrand
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** RED app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a RED app icon on your home screen?**

From a product design perspective, red is problematic for a unified entertainment platform. The reason is that different verticals within your platform—sports betting, casino games, maybe esports—have different emotional tones. Red works for high-urgency sports betting but contradicts the relaxed exploration of casino games. A unified platform needs a neutral, sophisticated base color that can flex across different use cases.

---

**Q2: How does RED make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Red creates a single-note emotional experience: urgency. For a multi-product platform, that's limiting. When users need to seamlessly switch between placing a quick bet, browsing new slot games, and checking their account, red maintains the same high-intensity tone throughout. That's exhausting. A good product experience modulates intensity based on context. Red doesn't allow for that modulation.

---

**Q3: Would RED help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, recognition would be excellent. But the question for a unified platform isn't just recognition—it's mental association. If users associate your app with stress or urgency because of red, they won't default to it for casual browsing or relaxed entertainment. You'll get high engagement during high-intensity moments but low engagement during low-intensity moments. That hurts overall platform stickiness.

---

**Q4: Does RED give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, etc.)?**

Red gives me urgency, action, and intensity—but not versatility, sophistication, or calm exploration. For a platform offering everything from live sports betting to casual slots to account management, red is tonally narrow. Premium unified platforms like Spotify or Netflix use blacks or deep neutrals precisely because those colors work across different content types and user moods. Red locks you into one mood.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a RED app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

2. The product logic doesn't support red for a unified entertainment platform. Red signals single-purpose urgency, not multi-purpose versatility. If you were a sports-betting-only app, I'd rate it higher. But for a cross-entertainment platform, red is a strategic mismatch. You need a color that says "we do everything well," not "we do one thing intensely."

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about RED as our brand color for the app icon?**

Think about user flows: someone opens the app to place a quick bet (red works), then browses new casino games (red is too intense), then checks their balance (red creates unnecessary anxiety). Red optimizes for one flow while undermining the others. A dark, sophisticated base with context-sensitive accent colors would better serve a unified platform's diverse use cases. Red is too blunt an instrument.
