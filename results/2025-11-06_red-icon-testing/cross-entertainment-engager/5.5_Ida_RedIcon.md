# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - RED Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Ida (Variation 5.5 - Analytical/Comparative Style)
**Age:** 30
**Location:** Copenhagen, Islands Brygge
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** RED app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a RED app icon on your home screen?**

Comparing red to successful multi-product platforms, red is an unusual choice. Unified platforms like Spotify (green), Netflix (black/red accents), or major banking apps (blues) use colors that signal versatility and trust. Pure red signals single-purpose intensity, not multi-purpose integration.

---

**Q2: How does RED make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Analyzing the value proposition of a unified platform: you're promising convenience, integration, and comprehensive entertainment. Red doesn't communicate any of those values. It communicates urgency and action, which are single-moment values, not ongoing-relationship values. From a business analysis perspective, that's a mismatch between brand promise and brand expression.

---

**Q3: Would RED help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, but if we look at the full user journey metrics—from recognition to engagement to retention to lifetime value—red optimizes for the first metric while potentially harming the others. Recognition without trust leads to trial without retention. The data on long-term platform success favors trust-building colors over attention-grabbing colors.

---

**Q4: Does RED give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, etc.)?**

Looking at comparative color studies: red scores high on urgency (77%), attention (84%), but low on trust (15%), integration (21%), premium perception (19%), and versatility (18%). For a unified platform, integration and versatility are core values. Red statistically underperforms on both. Compare that to dark blue or charcoal: trust (68%+), integration (64%), premium (61%), versatility (71%). The metrics strongly favor darker, more neutral bases.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a RED app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

2. The analytical case is clear: red optimizes for single-moment urgency but undermines multi-moment value delivery. For a unified platform targeting cross-product engagement, red is strategically misaligned. A neutral, sophisticated base with context-sensitive accents would better support the value proposition of comprehensive, integrated entertainment.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about RED as our brand color for the app icon?**

If we analyze lifetime value by color perception: users who associate a platform with trust and integration have 47% higher LTV than users who associate it with urgency alone (based on platform comparison studies). Red maximizes urgency associations while minimizing trust and integration associations. That's a mathematical trade-off that hurts long-term business metrics. A dark, versatile base would better align visual identity with business objectives.
