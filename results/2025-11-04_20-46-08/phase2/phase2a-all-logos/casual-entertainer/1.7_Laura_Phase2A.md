## Q6: TOP 3 RANKING

**Question:** Rank your top 3 favorite logos (1st, 2nd, 3rd) with reasoning.

**Laura's Response:**

**1st Place: Logo 2**
Most functional option. Excellent readability at all sizes - critical for mobile use. No unnecessary design elements that could complicate rendering or reduce clarity. The lowercase approach is modern and approachable without sacrificing professionalism. From a usability standpoint, this checks all boxes: clear, scalable, versatile, and professional. Would work equally well as an app icon, in notifications, or in promotional materials. Efficient design.

**2nd Place: Logo 4**
The shadow effect adds visual interest while maintaining readability. More eye-catching than Logo 2, which has practical benefits for initial discovery. My concern is whether the shadow effect scales well to small sizes - might cause legibility issues on notification icons. But overall, it's a functional design that balances impact with clarity. Would need to test at actual icon sizes to confirm usability.

**3rd Place: Logo 1**
The bold treatment ensures readability, which is important. The tilt adds energy, though I worry it might reduce clarity at smaller sizes or create balance issues in certain layouts. Functionally, it works well enough for most applications. My reservation is that the tilt is primarily stylistic rather than functional - it doesn't improve usability, just adds character. But it's still readable and clear, which matters most.

## Q7: IMMEDIATE ELIMINATIONS

**Question:** Which logos would you eliminate immediately? Why?

**Laura's Response:**

**Logo 7** - Fails basic readability test. If I can't immediately identify what it says, it's not functional. From a usability perspective, this creates friction. When you're on a crowded bus trying to find an app quickly, you don't have time to decode abstract designs. Eliminated for poor functionality.

**Logo 3 and Logo 6** - The script styles reduce readability, especially at small sizes. Cursive letters can blur together on lower-resolution screens or at small icon sizes. Not practical for mobile apps where clarity is essential. These might work for print materials, but functionally they're poor choices for digital applications. Both eliminated for usability concerns.

**Logo 5** - The thick outline makes it feel heavy and reduces the actual letter visibility. From a design efficiency standpoint, you're using visual weight on the outline rather than on clear letterforms. At small sizes, this would likely create a blocky shape that's harder to read. Not optimal from a functional perspective.

The practical choices are Logos 2, 4, and 1 - all maintain readability and functionality while offering different levels of visual impact.
