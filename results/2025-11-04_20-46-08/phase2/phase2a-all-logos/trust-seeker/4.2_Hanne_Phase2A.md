## Phase 2A Questions & Responses

**Question:** Which logo do you like the most overall? Why?

**Hanne's Response:**
"Logo 2, without question. The reason I prefer this one is because when you consider what a gambling platform needs to communicate, it's not excitement or flash - it's reliability and transparency. Logo 2 has clean typography that suggests organized systems, clear terms, and professional operations. As someone who tracks every transaction and reads every terms document, I need visual cues that the platform takes compliance seriously. Logo 2 provides those cues. The lowercase letters feel approachable but not casual, modern but not trendy. It looks like a platform that would have proper documentation, clear RTP disclosure, and Spillemyndigheden compliance front and center."

**Key Themes:**
- Reliability and transparency prioritized
- Clean typography = organized systems
- Professional compliance signals
- Balance of approachable and serious
- Documentation and disclosure expectations

### Q2: Trust Leader (Forced Choice)
**Question:** Which one feels most trustworthy for a betting/gambling platform?

**Hanne's Response:**
"Logo 2 is the clear trust leader, and I can explain exactly why. First, the clean sans-serif typography suggests digital professionalism and modern banking-level security. Second, there are no decorative elements that could distract from the serious business of handling people's money. Third, it doesn't try to create false excitement - gambling should be controlled entertainment, not hysteria.

Looking at the others: Logo 3 and Logo 6 with their script styles immediately signal 'entertainment first, safety second' - that's backwards for responsible gambling. Logo 4's bold shadow effect feels aggressive and suggests they might use aggressive retention tactics. Logo 1's tilted angle makes me question if their accounting is also 'tilted' - I know that sounds silly, but visual metaphors matter in trust building. Logo 5 is close to Logo 2 but the bold outline feels slightly more 'salesy' than trustworthy. Logo 7 is professional but perhaps too minimal - it doesn't communicate enough warmth for a consumer-facing brand.

Logo 2 strikes the perfect balance: professional enough to trust with financial transactions, modern enough to suggest updated security practices, and approachable enough to feel like they value customers. That's what trust looks like."

**Trust Criteria Applied:**
1. Professional typography = security standards
2. No distracting decoration = focus on safety
3. Controlled presentation = responsible gambling
4. Visual metaphors matter (tilted = questionable)
5. Balance of professional + approachable = customer value

**Systematic Evaluation:**

If I were advising Yes Games from a marketing perspective: you want to stand out to the right people. ### Q5: Download Intent (Forced Choice)
**Question:** If this were a betting app you'd never heard of, which logo would make you most likely to download or try it first?

**Hanne's Response:**
"Logo 2, and my decision process would be methodical. When I discover a new betting platform, I follow a specific evaluation sequence:

**Step 1: Visual First Impression (Logo)**
Logo 2 passes immediately because it suggests professional operations. Logos 3, 6, 1 would be filtered out instantly for appearing too casual or gimmicky.

**Step 2: App Store Research**
If Logo 2 gets me to the app page, I'm already primed positively to read reviews and check ratings. Visual professionalism creates a halo effect - I'm more likely to trust positive reviews if the logo already signals quality.

**Step 3: Licensing Check**
I'd look for Spillemyndigheden licensing. Logo 2's professional appearance makes me expect they have proper licensing, so if it's there, it confirms my positive impression. If a casual-looking logo has licensing, I'd be pleasantly surprised but still skeptical about their operational professionalism.

**Step 4: Terms & Conditions Review**
Logo 2 sets expectations that T&Cs will be clear and fair. I'd download to verify.

**Step 5: Test Deposit**
Only after all the above would I deposit - and Logo 2 has made it this far because it cleared every psychological hurdle.

Logos 4, 5, or 7 might get me to Step 2, but they'd face more scrutiny. Logos 1, 3, 6 wouldn't make it past Step 1.

So yes, Logo 2 would trigger my download because it promises the transparency and professionalism I need to trust a new gambling platform with my money."

**Decision Architecture:**
1. Visual filter (immediate pass/fail)
2. Halo effect on research phase
3. Licensing expectation setting
4. Terms clarity assumptions
5. Progressive commitment
6. Logo as first barrier to overcome

### Q7: Immediate Eliminations (Open)
**Question:** Which logos would you eliminate immediately? Why?

**Hanne's Response:**
"I would eliminate four logos immediately, and I can explain the specific trust failures for each:

**Logo 1 (Tilted stamp): Eliminated**
Reason: The tilted angle creates visual instability, which subconsciously suggests operational instability. When I'm trusting a platform with financial transactions, I need everything to feel 'squared away' and straight. Additionally, the stamp aesthetic feels gimmicky - like they're trying to create artificial authenticity rather than earning it through transparent practices. The tilt also makes it harder to read at smaller sizes, suggesting they prioritized creative expression over user experience. These are red flags for how they might run their platform.

**Logo 3 (Script): Eliminated**
Reason: Script typography is designed to evoke elegance and celebration, which are completely wrong for a gambling platform that should emphasize responsible gaming. This looks like a luxury goods brand or wedding service, not a licensed financial transaction platform. The script style also suggests 'traditional' in a way that makes me question if their security practices are outdated. Furthermore, script fonts are notorious for poor digital readability, especially on mobile - if they can't get basic UX right on their logo, what does that say about their platform design? Trust failure on multiple levels.

**Logo 6 (Rounded script): Eliminated**
Reason: Same core issues as Logo 3, but worse because the rounded treatment adds a 'cute' factor that's completely inappropriate. Gambling involves real money and real risk - treating it cutely is irresponsible. This logo would make me question if they have adequate responsible gambling tools, clear odds disclosure, and proper player protection measures. The visual language says 'don't take this seriously,' which is exactly the wrong message. Immediate elimination for suggesting they don't take their regulatory obligations seriously.

**Logo 4 (Bold shadow): Eliminated**
Reason: The bold shadow effect is visually aggressive in a way that triggers my skepticism about aggressive business practices. Shadows in design create artificial depth and drama - in a gambling context, this makes me think they might use manipulative retention tactics, unclear bonus terms, or aggressive marketing. The effect also feels dated, like early 2000s web design, suggesting their platform technology might also be outdated. Trust requires clarity and straightforwardness; Logo 4 is trying too hard to create impact, which makes me suspicious of what they're trying to distract from.

**Summary of Eliminations:**
All four eliminated logos fail the fundamental trust test: they prioritize aesthetic effect over clear, professional communication. In gambling, style over substance is a red flag. I need visual evidence of operational maturity, regulatory compliance, and customer-first design. None of these four provide that evidence."

**Systematic Elimination Criteria:**
1. Visual stability = operational stability
2. Appropriate aesthetic for financial services
3. Digital usability = platform quality proxy
4. Responsible gambling tone
5. Security practice implications
6. Manipulation risk assessment
7. Technology currency indicators

**Professional Skepticism Applied:**
- Each design choice evaluated for trust implications
- Psychological effects considered
- Operational practices inferred from visual choices
