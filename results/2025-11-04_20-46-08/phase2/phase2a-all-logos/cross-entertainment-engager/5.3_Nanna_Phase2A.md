## Phase 2A Questions & Responses

### Q1: Overall Preference (Forced Choice)
**Question:** Which logo do you like the most overall? Why?

**Nanna's Response:**
Logo 2, but I'm skeptical of any logo claiming to work across betting, casino, and esports. Most platforms have great branding and terrible integration. That said, Logo 2 at least doesn't oversell itself - it's simple enough that it won't clash with different product UIs. The lowercase treatment is neutral, which is actually what you need when you're trying to serve multiple contexts. My concern is whether the company behind it can actually deliver on the unified experience, because the logo alone can't fix fragmented product architecture.

---

### Q2: Trust Leader (Forced Choice)
**Question:** Which one feels most trustworthy for a betting/gambling platform?

**Nanna's Response:**
Logo 2 feels most trustworthy, though I'm skeptical about trust signals from logos alone. From a UX perspective, trust comes from consistent behavior, not visual design. But if we're evaluating purely on appearance, Logo 2 communicates professionalism without gimmicks. The others either try too hard (Logos 4, 5) or feel too casual (Logos 3, 6). Logo 7 hides behind abstraction, which in gambling contexts often signals they're trying to obscure something.

---

### Q3: Standout/Visibility (Forced Choice)
**Question:** Which one would stand out most in an app store or advertisement?

**Nanna's Response:**
Logo 4, clearly. But here's the problem: standout doesn't equal good UX. In my research, I've seen flashy logos attached to poorly designed products all the time. The shadow effect grabs attention, sure, but it's going to create implementation nightmares across different UI contexts. How does that shadow render on a dark mode interface? On a live sports betting screen with constantly changing odds? It'll either dominate the UI or get lost entirely. Standout is easy. Integration is hard.

---

### Q4: Brand Fit (Forced Choice)
**Question:** Which one best represents "Play Made Easy"?

**Nanna's Response:**
Logo 2, though I'm skeptical of the "easy" claim when platforms try to combine sports, casino, and esports. From a UX standpoint, "easy" means reduced cognitive load and clear information architecture. Logo 2's simplicity suggests they might understand this - it doesn't add visual noise. But most platforms fail at "easy" because they prioritize engagement metrics over actual usability. The logo hints at the right direction, but the tagline is doing a lot of work that the actual product experience needs to deliver.

---

### Q6: Top 3 Ranking (Ranked Choice)
**Question:** Rank your top 3 favorite logos (1st, 2nd, 3rd). Briefly explain your reasoning for each.

**Nanna's Response:**

**1st Place: Logo 2 (Refined lowercase)**
It's the least problematic from a UX integration perspective. The letterforms are functional, scalable, and won't compete with UI elements. My skepticism isn't about the logo itself - it's fine - but about whether the platform behind it can actually deliver unified experience. Most can't. The logo at least doesn't promise things it can't deliver visually.

**2nd Place: Logo 4 (Bold shadow)**
Strong visual presence, good for marketing. But I rank it second because I've seen too many platforms choose visually bold logos and then struggle with consistent implementation. That shadow is going to be a maintenance nightmare across sports betting interfaces, casino lobbies, and esports sections. Different contexts will require different treatments, which fragments the brand - exactly what unified platforms should avoid.

**3rd Place: Logo 1 (Tilted stamp)**
The playfulness works for casino entertainment, but I'm skeptical it scales to serious sports betting contexts. Users switching from placing a live bet on a football match to playing slots need visual consistency, not jarring tonal shifts. The tilt creates personality, which is good for marketing, but might create friction in actual product use.

---

### Q7: Immediate Eliminations (Open)
**Question:** Which logos would you eliminate immediately? Why?

**Nanna's Response:**
Logos 3 and 6 are immediate eliminations. Script typography is a UX disaster waiting to happen - illegible at small sizes, doesn't work in UI components, creates accessibility issues. Any platform claiming unified experience but choosing script logos is signaling they haven't thought through the actual user journey. These would fail the most basic usability tests.

Logo 7 (YS monogram) eliminates itself because abstraction in gambling creates distrust. Users need clear brand identity when handling money. Monograms work for luxury fashion, not betting platforms. It also creates a learning curve - users have to remember what "YS" means, which adds cognitive load. For a unified platform trying to serve multiple entertainment types, you need immediate brand recognition, not a puzzle to solve.

Logo 5 (bold outline) is borderline - the heavy borders would dominate small UI elements and clash with interface components. It's trying too hard to be noticed instead of being functional.

All these eliminations come down to the same UX principle: unified platforms need logos that enhance usability, not create implementation problems.
