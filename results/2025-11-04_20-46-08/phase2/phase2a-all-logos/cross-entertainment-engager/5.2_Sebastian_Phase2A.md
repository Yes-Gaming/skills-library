## Phase 2A Questions & Responses

### Q1: Overall Preference (Forced Choice)
**Question:** Which logo do you like the most overall? Why?

**Sebastian's Response:**
Logo 2, because when you think about a unified platform spanning sports betting, casino games, and social features, you need a logo that maintains consistency across all those different contexts. The refined lowercase design has a modular quality that works whether it's appearing in a sports betting interface, a casino game lobby, or in push notifications. The letterforms are distinct enough to be recognizable at small sizes - which is critical when users are switching between different sections of the app - but still sophisticated enough to convey the premium, integrated experience we're promising. The way it's designed, you can imagine it scaling from a small app icon to a large hero image on a TV app without losing its essence.

### Q2: Trust Leader (Forced Choice)
**Question:** Which one feels most trustworthy for a betting/gambling platform?

**Sebastian's Response:**
Logo 2. The reason is that trust in a multi-vertical platform requires consistency above all else. Users need to feel they're dealing with the same company whether they're placing a sports bet or playing slots. The refined lowercase has that professional uniformity - it doesn't over-promise with flashy elements, but it also doesn't feel cheap or casual. In product terms, this is the kind of mark that suggests the company has invested in proper infrastructure, proper integration, proper security across all verticals.

### Q6: Top 3 Ranking (Ranked Choice)
**Question:** Rank your top 3 favorite logos (1st, 2nd, 3rd). Briefly explain your reasoning for each.

**Sebastian's Response:**

**1st Place: Logo 2 (Refined lowercase)**
This is the clear winner from a product perspective. It solves the core challenge of a unified platform: maintaining brand consistency across multiple contexts while remaining functional at every touchpoint. The letterforms work in navigation bars, as app icons, in promotional materials, and even as small badges or indicators within the product. When you're building an ecosystem, every element needs to pull double duty, and this logo delivers on that requirement. It's sophisticated enough for high-stakes sports betting but approachable enough for casual casino gaming.

**2nd Place: Logo 4 (Bold shadow)**
Strong secondary choice because it excels at awareness and visibility, which matters for user acquisition. The dimensional quality gives it shelf appeal in app stores and makes it memorable in advertising. My concern from a product integration standpoint is that the shadow effect could create challenges in UI implementation - you'd need to carefully manage how it appears over different background colors, in different sections of the app, and at different sizes. But for external marketing, it's powerful.

**3rd Place: Logo 1 (Tilted stamp)**
I appreciate the playful energy of the tilted stamp, and it does communicate entertainment value effectively. The reason it ranks third is that the tilt, while distinctive, could become limiting in certain product contexts - imagine trying to align this with UI elements or integrate it into a clean, minimal interface design. It works well for casino-focused contexts but might feel out of place in serious sports betting interfaces. For a truly unified platform, you need more adaptability.

---

### Q7: Immediate Eliminations (Open)
**Question:** Which logos would you eliminate immediately? Why?

**Sebastian's Response:**
I'd eliminate Logos 3, 6, and 7 right away. Logos 3 and 6 (the script variations) fail the scalability test - script typography becomes illegible at small sizes, which is a fundamental problem when you need your logo to work as a 1024px app icon, a 32px navigation element, and a tiny notification badge. From a product perspective, this creates inconsistent brand presence across the user journey. Users might not even recognize it's the same app when switching contexts.

Logo 7 (the YS monogram) eliminates itself for different reasons - it's reductive rather than integrative. When you're building a unified platform, you want a mark that feels expansive, not contracted. The monogram feels like it's hiding the brand rather than expressing it. In user testing, abbreviated marks often create confusion, especially for newer users who haven't built brand familiarity. You'd end up needing the full wordmark alongside it in most contexts anyway, which defeats the purpose of having a simplified mark.

All three fail the fundamental product requirement: they don't scale across the ecosystem of touchpoints a multi-vertical platform requires.
