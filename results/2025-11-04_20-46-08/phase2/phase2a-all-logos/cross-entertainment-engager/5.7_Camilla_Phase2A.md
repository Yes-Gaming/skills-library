### Q6: Top 3 Ranking (Ranked Choice)
**Question:** Rank your top 3 favorite logos (1st, 2nd, 3rd). Briefly explain your reasoning for each.

**Camilla's Response:**

**1st Place: Logo 2 (Refined lowercase)**
Most practical choice for unified platform. From a usability standpoint, it checks all the boxes: legible at small sizes (important for mobile), works on any background color (important for diverse interface contexts), loads quickly (simple shapes), and doesn't distract from functionality. When you're switching between betting on a match, playing a slot game, and checking esports odds, you need consistency. Logo 2 provides that without getting in the way. It's efficient. Does it work across all use cases? Yes. Is it maintainable across different platform sections? Yes. That's what matters.

**2nd Place: Logo 4 (Bold shadow)**
Strong visibility, good for recognition. Functionally, it works for marketing purposes. My concern from a usability perspective is implementation - that shadow effect needs careful handling across different backgrounds and contexts. Is it practical for a sports betting interface with live odds? Maybe. For a dark-themed casino lobby? Would need testing. For small notification icons? Probably loses effectiveness. Logo 4 optimizes for one function (attention) but may create inefficiencies in others. Still, visibility has practical value for user acquisition.

**3rd Place: Logo 1 (Tilted stamp)**
Has personality and energy, which works for entertainment. Functionally, the tilt might create alignment challenges in UI design - harder to integrate into navigation bars, headers, and compact spaces. From a practical standpoint, angular elements are more complex to implement consistently than straight elements. But it's distinctive, and distinctiveness has practical value for brand recall. My question is: does the personality benefit outweigh the implementation costs? For third place, it's acceptable, but it's not as functionally efficient as Logo 2.

### Q7: Immediate Eliminations (Open)
**Question:** Which logos would you eliminate immediately? Why?

**Camilla's Response:**
Logos 3 and 6 eliminated immediately. Practically speaking, script fonts are usability disasters. They don't scale down well, they're hard to read quickly, and they create implementation problems across different contexts. When you're managing a unified platform with sports betting (requires fast information processing), casino games (needs clear navigation), and esports (real-time data display), you can't afford typography that slows users down. Do these work functionally? No. Are they efficient? Absolutely not. They prioritize aesthetics over usability.

Logo 7 (YS monogram) eliminated for practical reasons - it's not immediately clear what it represents. When users are switching between different entertainment types, they need clear brand orientation. Abstracting to initials adds cognitive load. Is it efficient communication? No. Does it work for quick recognition in navigation? Not as well as a full wordmark. Practically speaking, monograms work when you have established brand awareness, not when you're trying to communicate a unified platform concept.

Logo 5 (bold outline) is borderline eliminate - functionally, those heavy borders would dominate small UI spaces and create visual noise. When you're trying to build a clean, efficient interface across multiple entertainment contexts, elements that demand too much attention become usability problems. The outline adds weight without adding clarity. Is it functionally efficient? Not really.

All eliminations come down to the same practical assessment: do they work efficiently across all use cases? The answer is no.
