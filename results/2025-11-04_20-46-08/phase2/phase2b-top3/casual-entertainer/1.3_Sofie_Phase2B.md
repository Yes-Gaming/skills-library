## PHASE 2B: TOP 3 FINALISTS COMPARISON

**Context:** These are the top 3 logo finalists for Yes Games. Compare them carefully.

**Logos Presented (randomized order for this respondent):**

**Logo A:** Bold "YES" in tilted rectangular stamp/sticker style
**Logo B:** Lowercase "yes" refined, clean, modern
**Logo C:** Bold "YES" with shadow effect

---

## Test Responses

### Q1: Which of these three is your clear winner? Why?

Logo B, but I'm skeptical of all three honestly.

Logo B at least doesn't feel like it's trying to manipulate me with visual tricks. The lowercase thing is trendy right now - I see it everywhere in tech branding - but at least it's clean and not aggressive. The other two feel like they're trying too hard to create excitement or urgency, which is exactly the kind of psychological manipulation I hate in gambling marketing.

Logo A with the tilt feels like it's screaming "FUN! EXCITING! PLAY NOW!" which makes me distrust it immediately. Logo C with the shadow is doing the same thing, just less obviously. Both feel designed to trigger impulse decisions rather than informed choices.

### Q2: Which feels most trustworthy?

Logo B, but that's a low bar.

It's the only one that doesn't use visual tricks. No tilting, no shadows, no effects. Just clear typography. I'm studying marketing, so I know how brands use design elements to manipulate perception. Shadows create artificial depth to make things feel more "premium" or "real." Tilts create energy and urgency to encourage impulsive behavior. These are classic manipulation tactics.

Logo B feels more honest because it's not using those tricks. It's just... a wordmark. That said, I don't fully trust any gambling brand, so "most trustworthy" is relative.

### Q3: Which feels most modern and premium?

Logo B feels most modern. Premium? I'm not sure gambling should feel "premium" - that word feels designed to make people spend more money.

But objectively, yes, Logo B has that modern tech aesthetic. Clean, minimal, refined. It's the same visual language as Stripe, Airbnb, Notion - companies that want to feel approachable and sophisticated. Whether that's appropriate for a gambling app is another question.

Logo C with the shadow feels dated - very 2000s graphic design. Logo A feels like Instagram sticker culture, which is modern in one sense but not in a "premium" sense.

### Q4: Which fits iGaming/betting best?

This is where I have mixed feelings.

Logo C probably fits iGaming best in terms of what people expect. Gambling brands typically use bold, aggressive design with effects and high contrast. Logo C matches those expectations.

But should Yes Games match those expectations? That's the strategic question. If their tagline is "Play Made Easy" and they want to differentiate from sketchy gambling sites, maybe they should deliberately NOT look like typical iGaming.

Logo B breaks from category norms, which could either be smart differentiation or could make people not recognize it as a gambling platform. As a marketing student, I'd say it depends on their target audience and positioning strategy.

I'd probably say Logo C fits the category, but Logo B might be the smarter choice if they're trying to attract users who are turned off by typical gambling aesthetics.

### Q5: Rank all 3 (1st, 2nd, 3rd) with reasoning

**1st Place: Logo B (Lowercase "yes" refined)**

It's the most honest of the three. No visual manipulation, no effects designed to trigger urgency or excitement. Clean, readable, modern. If I'm going to use a gambling app - which I barely do because I don't trust the industry - I'd rather use one that doesn't try to psychologically manipulate me with its branding. Logo B feels less predatory, which in the gambling space is actually a meaningful difference.

The lowercase is friendly without being childish. The refinement shows attention to detail, which suggests the company might also pay attention to details like fair odds and responsible gambling features. It's not perfect, but it's the least problematic of the three.

**2nd Place: Logo C (Bold "YES" with shadow)**

It's problematic, but at least it's straightforward about what it is. The boldness and shadow scream "gambling!" which is honest in a way. I don't like the shadow effect because I know it's designed to make the logo feel more substantial and premium, which is a classic upselling tactic. The all-caps feels aggressive, like it's shouting at me.

That said, it's less gimmicky than Logo A. The shadow is a standard design technique, even if I don't like its psychological purpose. It would work functionally across different contexts. It's traditional gambling aesthetic, which means people would immediately know what the app is for. For better or worse, it matches category expectations.

**3rd Place: Logo A (Bold "YES" tilted stamp)**

I really don't like this one. The tilt feels manipulative - it's designed to create energy, excitement, and a sense of playfulness to encourage impulse behavior. That's exactly the kind of design tactic that makes gambling apps problematic.

The stamp/sticker aesthetic feels like it's trying to be trendy and appeal to younger users, which concerns me because younger users are more vulnerable to gambling addiction. It feels like it's designed to make gambling feel casual and fun rather than serious, which downplays the risks.

From a purely design perspective, the tilt makes it harder to read quickly and would be awkward in many contexts. The rectangular container is unnecessary and limits versatility. It's the most gimmicky of the three, and gimmicks in gambling branding make me suspicious of what they're trying to distract from.

### Q6: What makes your #1 better than your #2?

Logo B doesn't try to manipulate my emotions and behavior through design tricks. Logo C absolutely does - that shadow is there to create artificial depth and weight, making the logo feel more "real" or "substantial," which is a psychological tactic to build unearned trust or perceived value.

As someone studying marketing, I'm hyperaware of these tactics, and they make me distrust brands that use them. I know the gambling industry uses every psychological trick in the book to encourage spending and engagement. When I see a brand that restrains itself from using obvious manipulation techniques, I trust it slightly more.

Logo B is honest. It's just typography. It doesn't try to create false urgency, artificial excitement, or unearned premium feelings. In an industry full of predatory design, restraint is actually a differentiator.

Also practically, Logo B is more versatile. It would work better at small sizes, on different backgrounds, and in different contexts. Logo C's shadow could look muddy or disappear depending on the background. That kind of design thinking - or lack thereof - makes me question whether the company thinks through details in other areas, like responsible gambling features or fair odds.

## Key Takeaways (Interviewer Notes)

- Highly critical lens on design psychology and manipulation tactics
- Recognizes and calls out visual techniques designed to influence behavior
- Values honesty and restraint over excitement and category conventions
- Skeptical of all gambling branding but least skeptical of simplest option
- Marketing education informs critical analysis of design intentions
- Connects design choices to broader concerns about industry ethics

**Winner:** Logo B (Lowercase "yes" refined)
**Primary Reason:** Most honest, least manipulative, no visual tricks, shows restraint in predatory industry
