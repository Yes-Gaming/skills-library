# YES GAMES LOGO TESTING - SURVEY RESPONSES

## LOGO 5 (Bold "Yes" with black outline)

**A. Emotional & Intuitive Response**

A1. **First word/feeling:** Aggressive
A2. **What kind of company:** Mobile gaming app trying too hard to stand out
A3. **Trust level:** 3/5 - The heavy-handed approach makes me question if they're compensating for something
A4. **Modern or dated:** 4/5 - Modern but feels optimized for app store conversion rather than actual user experience
A5. **Premium/mass-market/budget:** Mass-market, possibly budget

**C. Fit & Relevance**

C1. **Fit for iGaming/betting/entertainment:** 3/5 - It fits superficially but doesn't address deeper UX needs
C2. **Target audience:** Users who respond to loud, immediate visual signals - not necessarily sophisticated users

**D. Open Commentary**

D1. **What works/doesn't work:** This is a classic case of designing for acquisition rather than retention. The thick black outline exists for one reason: to maximize visibility in app store listings and on crowded home screens. That's a growth hacking decision, not a user experience decision. And here's the problem - once you've acquired the user with this bold, loud branding, what happens when they're inside your supposedly unified entertainment platform? That same aggressive outline doesn't suddenly become subtle and sophisticated. It persists across every touchpoint, and for a platform claiming to integrate sports betting, casino games, and esports, that one-note personality becomes exhausting. I'm skeptical that a company making this design choice has really thought through the full cross-platform experience. Show me the design system documentation. Show me how this logo adapts its presence across different contexts - sports statistics interface, casino game lobby, esports tournament brackets. I suspect it doesn't, because this was designed for conversion metrics, not for coherent UX across a unified ecosystem.

A3. **Trust level:** 2/5 - The excessive friendliness in gambling context raises red flags

**C. Fit & Relevance**

C1. **Fit for iGaming/betting/entertainment:** 1/5 - Completely wrong for the category
C2. **Target audience:** This seems designed to make gambling feel non-threatening, which is concerning from an ethical UX perspective

**D. Open Commentary**

D1. **What works/doesn't work:** I'm immediately skeptical of gambling platforms that present themselves with overly soft, friendly visual language. It feels like deliberate design to make users less cautious about spending money. The rounded, gentle script says "this is harmless fun!" when gambling objectively isn't harmless for everyone. From a UX research perspective, I'd want to see the thinking behind this choice. Is it trying to appeal to risk-averse users by reducing perceived threat? It completely undermines the sports betting aspect - I can't take a betting interface seriously with this logo. The contrast issues (especially white on yellow) show lack of attention to accessibility standards. And the script typography will fail at small sizes. This appears designed to make users comfortable rather than to serve their actual needs across a complex entertainment ecosystem.
