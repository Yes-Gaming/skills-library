# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Blue Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Caroline (Variation 3.5 - Analytical/Comparative Style)
**Age:** 29
**Location:** Copenhagen, Østerbro
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Blue app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a blue app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

Statistically speaking, blue is one of the most common app icon colors, used by approximately 30-35% of popular apps. In the casino gaming category specifically, blue is an outlier—most casino apps use warmer, more vibrant colors. My first reaction is that this is attempting to differentiate through trust signaling rather than entertainment signaling.

---

**Q2: How does blue make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

If we compare color psychology data, blue consistently scores high for trust (85%+) but low for excitement and entertainment (under 30%). Casino gaming is an entertainment category driven by reward anticipation and excitement, so there's a mathematical mismatch between the emotional profile blue creates and the emotional profile that drives casino app engagement.

That said, if the target market is risk-averse players who need trust reassurance before engaging, blue could optimize for conversion at the top of the funnel even if it underperforms on excitement metrics.

---

**Q3: Would blue help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

The data suggests no. Blue has high frequency across multiple categories—social media (Facebook, LinkedIn, Twitter), communication (Messages, Skype), productivity (Outlook), and finance (PayPal, Chase). From a recognition efficiency standpoint, blue would rank in approximately the 35th percentile for distinctiveness—meaning 65% of other color options would perform better for rapid identification.

---

**Q4: Does blue give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

Looking at color association research, blue scores as follows:
- Trust: 85-90% (highest)
- Professionalism: 80-85%
- Calmness: 75-80%
- Energy: 20-25% (very low)
- Fun: 25-30% (very low)
- Excitement: 15-20% (very low)

For premium vs. cheap, blue is neutral—dependent on shade and execution. Navy blue can signal premium; bright blue can feel more accessible.

The critical insight is that blue optimizes for trust at the significant expense of entertainment value, which is the core value proposition of casino apps.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a blue app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

2.5. From a pure mathematics standpoint, blue is suboptimal for the casino category. However, if Yes Games has data showing that trust is the primary barrier to entry for their target demographic—specifically if they're targeting older or more conservative users who are skeptical of casino apps—then blue becomes more defensible despite its poor entertainment metrics.

Without demographic prioritization data, choosing blue is choosing trust over engagement, which seems strategically misaligned for a category where engagement drives revenue.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about blue as our brand color for the app icon?**

The strategic question is whether Yes Games is optimizing for a trust-seeking niche or for mainstream casino entertainment. Blue positions the app in the trust-seeking niche, which may have higher LTV but lower volume. If the business model requires high-volume, high-frequency users, blue is statistically the wrong choice.

I'd want to see A/B test data comparing blue against warmer colors (red, purple, gold) measuring both download rates and retention/engagement metrics. My hypothesis is that blue would perform better on trust perception surveys but worse on actual usage frequency, which is the metric that matters for casino apps.
