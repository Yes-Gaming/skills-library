**Persona:** Viktor (Variation 3.2 - Detailed/Explanatory Style)
**Age:** 28
**Location:** Aarhus, Trøjborg
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Blue app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a blue app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

From a design perspective, blue is technically sound—it's universally readable, accessible, and has strong contrast potential. But the reason I'm hesitant is because blue doesn't align with the visual language of casino gaming. When you look at successful casino apps, they typically use richer, more luxurious colors—purples, golds, blacks, deep reds—to signal entertainment and excitement. Blue feels like it's from a different category entirely.

---

**Q2: How does blue make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

The way I see it, blue creates cognitive dissonance in the casino context. Casino games are designed around visual excitement, dynamic animations, vibrant colors, and sensory stimulation. Blue is calming, stable, and professional—which are the opposite emotional triggers. Technically, this could work if you're positioning as a "responsible" or "serious" casino platform, but that's swimming against the category expectations.

From a game design standpoint, blue would signal low arousal and low excitement, which contradicts the core appeal of casino gaming. The color psychology doesn't align with the user's emotional goals when they open a casino app.

---

**Q3: Would blue help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Not effectively. Blue has extremely high frequency in app icons across multiple categories—social media, productivity, communication, finance. From a visual hierarchy perspective, blue would rank in the bottom third for distinctiveness.

If you're designing for rapid visual identification—which is critical for entertainment apps where users want quick access—blue is a suboptimal choice. You'd need exceptionally strong icon design and branding to overcome the color's lack of category differentiation.

---

**Q4: Does blue give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

Blue's primary associations are trust, reliability, and professionalism. In color theory, it's classified as a "cool" color with low energy activation. That's excellent for financial services, healthcare, or corporate tools where you want users to feel secure and make rational decisions.

But in casino gaming, you're trying to create the opposite emotional state—excitement, energy, anticipation. The color should support the game experience, not work against it. Blue undermines the entertainment value proposition.

In terms of premium vs. cheap, blue can be premium if executed with sophistication—think luxury tech brands—but it requires very high-quality design execution. A poorly executed blue would feel generic rather than cheap.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a blue app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

Technically, I'd give it a 2.5. It's not fundamentally broken—blue can be made to work with exceptional design execution. But strategically, it's misaligned with category expectations and user psychology. If I were designing this as a game developer, I'd argue strongly against blue unless there's a very specific strategic reason (like targeting a trust-concerned demographic who are skeptical of traditional casino aesthetics).

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about blue as our brand color for the app icon?**

The question I'd ask is: what problem is blue solving? If the answer is "we need to differentiate from flashy, untrustworthy casino apps," then blue makes sense as a strategic counter-positioning. But if the goal is to appeal to casino game enthusiasts who are looking for visual excitement and entertainment, blue is working against you.

From a UX and game design perspective, the app icon should create anticipation and excitement—it's the first touchpoint of the entertainment experience. Blue creates the wrong emotional setup for what users expect when they open a casino app.
