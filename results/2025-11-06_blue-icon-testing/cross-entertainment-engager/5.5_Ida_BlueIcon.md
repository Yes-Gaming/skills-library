# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Blue Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Ida (Variation 5.5 - Analytical/Comparative Style)
**Age:** 30
**Location:** Copenhagen, Islands Brygge
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Blue app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a blue app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

When I compare blue to alternative positioning strategies for unified platforms, it's a clear signal toward trust and integration over excitement and engagement. From a business analyst perspective, blue suggests that Yes Games is prioritizing platform credibility over vertical-specific appeal.

---

**Q2: How does blue make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

If we analyze the value proposition of a unified entertainment platform, there are two possible positioning strategies:

1. Excitement-driven: "We have everything you love in one amazing place!"
2. Trust-driven: "We're your reliable, integrated entertainment solution"

Blue clearly optimizes for strategy #2. That's a defensible choice if the data shows that trust is the primary barrier to platform adoption, but it sacrifices the engagement and excitement that typically drive entertainment usage.

Compared to competitors who use warmer, more energetic colors, blue positions Yes Games as the "serious, trustworthy" option. That's a niche positioning that may have lower volume but higher retention.

---

**Q3: Would blue help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

From a visual recognition standpoint, blue scores poorly—approximately 35th percentile for distinctiveness due to high frequency across multiple app categories. However, if we analyze this from a platform usage perspective rather than a single-app perspective, recognition speed may be less critical.

Power users who rely on a unified platform likely access it frequently enough that recognition becomes habitual rather than visual-search-dependent. The question is whether Yes Games is targeting power users (where blue works) or casual users (where blue underperforms).

---

**Q4: Does blue give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

Looking at the data on color associations for platform brands:

**Trust & Reliability:**
- Blue scores 85%+ on trust perception
- Highest of any color for "reliable platform" association
- Strong correlation with integrated ecosystems

**Integration Signals:**
- Blue is commonly used by platform companies (Facebook/Meta, LinkedIn, Twitter/X)
- Signals "comprehensive solution" vs. "single-purpose tool"
- Appropriate for cross-vertical positioning

**Engagement Metrics:**
- Blue scores low (20-30%) on excitement and energy
- May reduce impulse usage but increase deliberate usage
- Better for retention than acquisition

The analytical insight is that blue optimizes for platform loyalty over usage frequency—which is a strategic trade-off that depends on the business model.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a blue app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

3.5. From a business analysis perspective, blue is appropriate if Yes Games' unit economics depend on high-LTV, low-churn users who value platform integration and reliability. Blue positions the platform as a trusted destination rather than an entertainment impulse.

However, if the business model requires high-frequency usage across multiple verticals to drive revenue, blue may suppress engagement velocity compared to warmer alternatives. The rating would be higher if I saw data showing that trust is the primary adoption barrier and that platform users have sufficient engagement once they overcome that barrier.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about blue as our brand color for the app icon?**

The strategic question is what metric Yes Games is optimizing for:

**If optimizing for trust and platform adoption (40+ demographic, high LTV):**
Blue is the correct choice. It signals reliability, integration, and professionalism—exactly what drives platform commitment for risk-averse users.

**If optimizing for engagement frequency and cross-vertical usage (18-35 demographic, high volume):**
Blue is suboptimal. It doesn't create the excitement and energy that drives frequent entertainment engagement.

I'd want to see the segmentation data and LTV analysis before making a final recommendation. Blue is a strategic positioning choice, not a neutral design decision—it explicitly targets one demographic at the expense of another. Yes Games needs to be clear about which segment drives their business model before committing to blue.
