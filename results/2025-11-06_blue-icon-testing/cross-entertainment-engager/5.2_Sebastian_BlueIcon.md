# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Blue Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Sebastian (Variation 5.2 - Detailed/Explanatory Style)
**Age:** 32
**Location:** Aarhus, Brabrand
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Blue app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a blue app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

From a product perspective, blue signals platform maturity and ecosystem thinking. When I see a blue icon, I think "this is a company that's thinking about trust, integration, and long-term user relationships" rather than "this is a flashy game." That's actually appropriate for a unified platform strategy because you're not selling individual games—you're selling a comprehensive entertainment ecosystem.

---

**Q2: How does blue make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

The way I see it, blue works well for a unified platform because it signals reliability and consistency across different entertainment verticals. When you're offering sports betting, casino games, and potentially other entertainment options all in one app, you need a color that says "everything here works together seamlessly and you can trust the experience across all features."

Blue communicates that platform cohesion. It says "this isn't a collection of random games thrown together—this is a thoughtfully integrated system." That aligns with what cross-platform users like me are looking for: one place where everything just works.

However, the trade-off is that blue doesn't capture the excitement of any individual vertical. It's a compromise color that works for everything but isn't optimal for any single category.

---

**Q3: Would blue help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

From a UX perspective, recognition speed is less critical for a unified platform than for a single-purpose app. When I'm using Yes Games, I'm not necessarily looking for instant gratification—I'm accessing my primary entertainment hub. Blue positions it as an essential utility (like my banking or email app) rather than an impulse-driven entertainment option.

That's actually strategic for a platform play. You want users to think of Yes Games as their default entertainment destination, not just "that fun game I play sometimes." Blue reinforces that positioning.

---

**Q4: Does blue give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

Blue strongly signals trust, reliability, and platform quality—all critical for a unified entertainment platform where users are managing accounts, making transactions, and engaging across multiple features. From a product standpoint, blue tells users "we've invested in making this a professional, integrated experience—not a scrappy MVP."

In terms of premium vs. cheap, blue can definitely be premium if executed well. Think about high-quality SaaS platforms, financial apps, or tech ecosystems—many use blue to signal sophistication and reliability. For a cross-platform entertainment app, that's the right positioning.

The weakness is that blue doesn't create excitement or urgency, which might reduce engagement frequency for casual users. But for power users who are already committed to the platform, trust and reliability matter more than momentary excitement.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a blue app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

I'd give it a 4. Blue is a smart choice for a unified platform strategy because it prioritizes trust, integration, and long-term user relationships over short-term excitement. That's the right trade-off if Yes Games is building a platform rather than just a collection of games.

The reason it's not a 5 is that blue might not appeal to casual users looking for entertainment rather than a committed platform relationship. But for power users and cross-entertainment engagers like me, blue signals exactly what I want: a reliable, integrated, professional ecosystem.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about blue as our brand color for the app icon?**

If Yes Games goes with blue, it needs to deliver on what blue promises: seamless integration, reliable performance across all features, professional customer service, and a cohesive design system. Blue creates expectations of platform quality—if the actual experience is fragmented or buggy, users will feel betrayed.

But if the execution matches the promise, blue is the right positioning for a unified entertainment platform. It says "we're not just another gaming app—we're your trusted entertainment destination." That's a strong value proposition for users who want everything in one place.
