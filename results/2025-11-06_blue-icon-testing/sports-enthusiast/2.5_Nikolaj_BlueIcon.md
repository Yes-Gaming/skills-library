# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Blue Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Nikolaj (Variation 2.5 - Analytical/Comparative Style)
**Age:** 34
**Location:** Copenhagen, Østerbro
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Blue app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a blue app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

Compared to the competitive landscape, blue is the dominant color choice in the betting industry. bet365 uses blue, William Hill uses blue, Paddy Power has blue elements—there's clear category convergence around this color. My first reaction is that this is strategically safe but lacks differentiation potential from a market positioning standpoint.

---

**Q2: How does blue make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

If we evaluate this analytically, blue optimizes for trust and credibility metrics, which are critical in the betting industry given regulatory scrutiny and user concerns about platform legitimacy. From a risk management perspective, blue reduces the perceived risk of engaging with the platform.

However, if we look at engagement metrics, blue underperforms compared to warmer colors in terms of emotional activation and excitement generation. For a betting app where user engagement and frequency of use drive revenue, that's a potential opportunity cost. Blue may increase trust but decrease engagement velocity.

---

**Q3: Would blue help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

From a pure information architecture perspective, no. Blue has the highest frequency among app icon colors, particularly in the finance, social media, and productivity categories. If we model recognition efficiency, blue would score in approximately the 40th percentile for distinctiveness—meaning 60% of other color choices would likely perform better for rapid visual identification.

The key question is whether the trust benefit of blue outweighs the recognition cost. That's a strategic trade-off that depends on which metric is more valuable to the business model.

---

**Q4: Does blue give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

Looking at the data from color psychology research and brand perception studies, blue consistently scores highest for trust (85-90% association rate), professionalism (80-85%), and security (75-80%). These are strong alignment metrics if the primary user concern is platform legitimacy.

On the other hand, blue scores significantly lower for energy (20-25%), excitement (15-20%), and fun (25-30%). If we compare that to the emotional profile required to drive betting behavior—which is often impulse-driven and excitement-seeking—there's a mismatch.

In terms of premium vs. cheap, blue is neutral and shade-dependent. Navy or cobalt can signal premium, while bright or electric blue may feel more accessible or casual.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a blue app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

From a quantitative standpoint, I'd rate it a 3.5. Blue scores well on trust and legitimacy metrics, which are non-negotiable in the betting industry. However, it underperforms on engagement and differentiation metrics, which drive user acquisition and retention.

If Yes Games' strategic priority is to maximize trust among risk-averse users—particularly the 40+ demographic with higher lifetime value—then blue is a solid choice. But if the priority is to drive volume and engagement among younger, more impulse-driven users, blue may be suboptimal.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about blue as our brand color for the app icon?**

The critical insight here is that blue is not a neutral choice—it's a strategic positioning statement. By choosing blue, Yes Games is signaling that it prioritizes trust, professionalism, and legitimacy over excitement, energy, and differentiation.

If I were evaluating this from a portfolio optimization perspective, I'd want to see the data on user acquisition cost, lifetime value, and churn rates by demographic segment. If the 40+ trust-seeking demographic has significantly higher LTV than the 18-30 excitement-seeking demographic, then blue is the correct financial decision even if it costs you volume.

But if the business model depends on high-frequency, lower-value users, blue may be leaving money on the table. I'd recommend A/B testing blue against at least two alternative colors (one warmer, one more distinctive) to validate the trust-vs-engagement trade-off with actual user data.
