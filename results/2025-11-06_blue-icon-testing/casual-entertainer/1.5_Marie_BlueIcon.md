# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Blue Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Marie (Variation 1.5 - Analytical/Comparative Style)
**Age:** 26
**Location:** Copenhagen, Østerbro
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Blue app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a blue app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

If we compare this to the market, blue is one of the most common app colors across categories. Looking at the data, approximately 33% of top apps use blue as their primary color. So my first reaction is that this is statistically safe but unremarkable.

---

**Q2: How does blue make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Compared to other gaming apps, blue is an outlier. Most entertainment apps use warmer colors—reds, oranges, yellows, purples—to signal excitement and energy. Blue, on the other hand, typically tests higher for trust and reliability but lower for excitement and engagement.

In the context of a gaming app, blue feels like it's optimizing for trust at the expense of entertainment appeal. That might be strategic if your target demographic values security over excitement, but for casual entertainment users like me, it's not particularly compelling.

---

**Q3: Would blue help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

The data suggests no. When I analyze my own home screen, I have multiple blue icons: Messages, Safari, LinkedIn, Twitter/X, and others. Blue has high frequency but low distinctiveness. From a recognition standpoint, colors like yellow, orange, or pink would likely perform 40-50% better in findability tests simply because they're less common.

---

**Q4: Does blue give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

Looking at color psychology research, blue consistently scores highest for trust (87% association rate), professionalism (82%), and calmness (79%). However, it scores much lower for energy (23%), fun (31%), and excitement (18%).

If we compare that to the emotional profile of successful gaming apps, there's a significant mismatch. Blue optimizes for the wrong emotional metrics for this category.

In terms of premium vs. cheap, blue is neutral—it depends entirely on shade and execution. A deep navy can feel premium; a bright cyan can feel generic.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a blue app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

3. From an analytical standpoint, blue is defensible but not optimal. It addresses trust concerns effectively but underperforms on engagement metrics that drive app usage in the entertainment category. If I were evaluating this as an A/B test scenario, I'd recommend testing blue against at least two warmer alternatives to validate the assumption that trust outweighs excitement for this user base.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about blue as our brand color for the app icon?**

The strategic question is: what are you optimizing for? If the data shows that trust is the primary barrier to download and usage, then blue makes sense—it directly addresses that concern. But if the data shows that engagement, excitement, or entertainment value is the driver, then blue is misaligned with user priorities.

I'd also want to see comparative testing data. How does blue perform against warmer colors in terms of download rates, app open frequency, and user retention? Without that data, blue feels like a safe default rather than an evidence-based choice.
