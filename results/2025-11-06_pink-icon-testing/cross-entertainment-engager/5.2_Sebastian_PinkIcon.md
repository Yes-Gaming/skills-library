# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Pink Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Sebastian (Variation 5.2 - Detailed/Explanatory Style)
**Age:** 32
**Location:** Aarhus, Brabrand
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Pink app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a pink app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

My first reaction is to evaluate it from a product strategy perspective. As a product manager, I think about how an icon communicates the value proposition. Pink is bold and distinctive, which could work for a focused product. But for a unified platform that aims to be the single destination for sports betting, casino gaming, and esports, pink feels like it's optimizing for the wrong attributes.

Pink signals playfulness and approachability, which are nice-to-haves. But a unified platform needs to signal comprehensiveness, reliability, and integration—those are the core value propositions. Pink doesn't deliver on that.

---

**Q2: How does pink make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Pink makes me feel like Yes Games is positioning itself as a casual, fun brand rather than a serious, comprehensive platform. And that's a strategic choice, but I'm not sure it's the right one for a multi-product ecosystem.

Let me explain with a product framework:

**Core Value Proposition of Unified Platform:**
1. **Convenience:** One app instead of three
2. **Integration:** Seamless experience across verticals
3. **Comprehensiveness:** Everything you need in one place
4. **Reliability:** Consistent quality across all products

**What Pink Communicates:**
1. **Fun:** Yes, pink signals this
2. **Approachability:** Yes, pink delivers this
3. **Playfulness:** Yes, absolutely

**What Pink Does NOT Communicate:**
1. **Comprehensiveness:** Pink feels narrow, not broad
2. **Integration:** Pink feels like one vibe, not multiple verticals working together
3. **Reliability:** Pink doesn't signal operational excellence
4. **Seriousness:** Pink undermines the "we do it all professionally" message

So there's a mismatch. Pink works if you're a single-purpose app (e.g., casual slot games). But for a unified platform, you need a color that signals breadth, stability, and integration. Blue or dark teal would work. Pink doesn't.

---

**Q3: Would pink help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, from a pure usability standpoint, pink would have excellent visibility. But visibility is table stakes. What matters more for a unified platform is whether users trust that opening this one app will meet all their needs.

Pink makes me think: "Oh, that's the fun gaming app." But I don't think: "Oh, that's the comprehensive platform where I can bet on sports, play casino games, and watch esports—all with a seamless experience."

Color should reinforce your core value proposition. Pink reinforces "fun," not "unified."

---

**Q4: Does pink give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

Let me map pink's associations against what a unified platform needs:

**Pink's Strengths:**
- Approachability: 9/10
- Fun: 8/10
- Differentiation: 9/10
- Memorability: 8/10

**Pink's Weaknesses (for unified positioning):**
- Comprehensiveness signal: 2/10 (critical for multi-product platforms)
- Integration signal: 3/10 (users need to believe verticals work together)
- Professional reliability: 3/10 (multi-product platforms need trust)
- Cross-vertical appeal: 4/10 (sports bettors, casino players, esports fans—do they all resonate with pink?)

**Competitive Benchmarking:**
Look at successful unified platforms in other categories:
- **Amazon:** Simple, professional design (everything store = professional presentation)
- **Google:** Multi-color (signals diversity and comprehensiveness)
- **Microsoft:** Professional blue (reliable, enterprise-grade, comprehensive)

None of these use pink because pink signals specificity, not breadth. Unified platforms need to signal "we do it all," not "we do one thing really well."

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a pink app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

**2.5 out of 5.**

Here's my product manager assessment:

**Pros (+1.5 points):**
- High differentiation in a crowded market
- Strong brand recall and memorability
- Appeals to younger, casual users

**Cons (-2.5 points):**
- Misaligned with unified platform positioning (major strategic error)
- Doesn't signal comprehensiveness or integration (core value props)
- Risk of alienating serious sports bettors or high-stakes casino players
- May create confusion about what the platform actually offers

I'd rate it higher if Yes Games were a single-vertical product (e.g., just casual slots). But for a unified platform, pink sends the wrong message. It makes users think you're a niche player, not a comprehensive solution.

**Q6: Any other thoughts about pink as our brand color for the app icon?**

A few strategic product considerations:

**1. Know your positioning before choosing your color.**

If Yes Games is:
- **A casual gaming app:** Pink works (signals fun, approachability)
- **A unified gambling platform:** Pink doesn't work (doesn't signal comprehensiveness)
- **A sports betting app:** Pink doesn't work (too soft for competitive energy)
- **A premium casino:** Pink doesn't work (doesn't signal premium)

So the first question isn't "Should we use pink?" It's "What are we positioning ourselves as?" The answer to that question determines your color strategy.

**2. Color should reinforce product strategy, not contradict it.**

If your product strategy is "be the unified platform for all gambling entertainment," your color should signal:
- Breadth (we have everything)
- Integration (it all works together)
- Reliability (you can depend on us)
- Professional quality (we do it all well)

Pink signals none of those things. It signals narrow, playful, casual. That's a product strategy contradiction.

**3. Test cross-vertical appeal.**

A unified platform needs to appeal to different user types:
- Sports bettors (competitive, analytical, masculine-coded)
- Casino players (entertainment-focused, varied demographics)
- Esports fans (young, tech-savvy, diverse)

Pink may appeal to some of these groups but alienate others. You'd need to test whether pink has cross-vertical appeal or whether it's polarizing.

My hypothesis: pink works for casino and esports, struggles with sports betting.

**4. Consider a more balanced approach.**

Instead of pure pink, consider:
- **Dark background with pink accents:** Signals comprehensive platform with modern differentiation
- **Multi-color icon:** Different colors for different verticals, unified by design system
- **Sophisticated purple or teal:** Modern and unique, but less polarizing than pink

These approaches balance differentiation with comprehensiveness signaling.

**Final Recommendation:**

As a product manager, I'd advise against pink for a unified platform. It's a strategic misalignment between your positioning (comprehensive, integrated, reliable) and your brand signal (playful, casual, narrow).

If you insist on differentiation through color, choose something that signals breadth and professionalism—like a unique shade of teal or a sophisticated dark purple. Those colors say "we're different AND we're comprehensive."

Pink says "we're different." But it doesn't say "we do it all." And for a unified platform, that's a critical gap.
