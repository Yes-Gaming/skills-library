# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Pink Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Nanna (Variation 5.3 - Skeptical/Critical Style)
**Age:** 28
**Location:** Copenhagen, Nørrebro
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Pink app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a pink app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

My first thought, as a UX researcher, is: "What problem does this solve?" Pink is visually distinctive, sure, but does it help users understand what Yes Games actually is? If you're positioning as a unified platform with multiple verticals, pink creates more questions than answers.

---

**Q2: How does pink make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Pink makes me skeptical that Yes Games has a coherent UX strategy. Here's why:

In UX, we design with intention. Every color choice should map to a user need or brand attribute. So I ask: what user need does pink serve?

- **If the need is visibility:** Pink works, but so do many other colors.
- **If the need is trust:** Pink fails—it doesn't signal trust for gambling.
- **If the need is comprehensiveness:** Pink fails—it signals playfulness, not breadth.
- **If the need is integration:** Pink fails—it doesn't communicate a unified experience.

Pink seems like a choice made for differentiation without considering whether it actually serves users. That's a UX red flag. It suggests the team prioritized "standing out" over "serving user needs."

---

**Q3: Would pink help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, technically. But as a UX researcher, I know that findability is only valuable if the app delivers on expectations. Pink sets an expectation of playfulness and casualness. If users open it expecting a fun, casual experience and instead find a complex multi-vertical platform, there's a UX disconnect.

Consistency between brand promise (icon) and product experience (app) is critical. Pink promises one thing; a unified platform delivers another. That's a usability problem.

---

**Q4: Does pink give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

From a UX research perspective, pink creates several cognitive mismatches:

**Association Mismatch #1: Simplicity vs. Complexity**
- Pink signals simplicity and singularity (one thing done well)
- Unified platforms are complex (multiple verticals integrated)
- Users expecting simplicity will be confused by complexity

**Association Mismatch #2: Casualness vs. Professionalism**
- Pink signals casual, fun experiences
- Unified platforms need to feel professional and reliable
- Users expecting casual fun may not trust serious gambling features

**Association Mismatch #3: Narrow vs. Broad**
- Pink feels specific and targeted (one niche)
- Unified platforms are broad and comprehensive (everything)
- Users may not understand the full offering

These mismatches create cognitive load and confusion. Good UX reduces cognitive load. Pink increases it.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a pink app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

**2 out of 5.**

From a UX perspective, pink scores poorly because it creates expectation mismatches and doesn't align with the core user need: understanding and trusting a unified gambling platform.

**UX Failures:**
- Doesn't communicate platform scope (users won't know it's multi-vertical)
- Doesn't signal professionalism (users may not trust it for serious gambling)
- Creates aesthetic-functional disconnect (playful appearance, complex functionality)
- May alienate key user segments (sports bettors, high-stakes casino players)

**Marginal UX Wins:**
- High visibility (faster app location)
- Memorable branding (easier recall)

But the failures outweigh the wins. A unified platform needs clarity, trust, and comprehensiveness signaling. Pink delivers none of those.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about pink as our brand color for the app icon?**

Several UX research recommendations:

**1. Test with actual users across all verticals.**

Don't assume pink works for everyone. Test specifically:
- **Sports bettors:** Does pink make them more or less likely to use Yes Games for sports betting?
- **Casino players:** Does pink appeal to them?
- **Esports fans:** Do they resonate with pink?

My hypothesis: Pink will polarize. Some segments love it, others hate it. For a unified platform, you need broad appeal, not polarization.

**2. Map color to user mental models.**

Users have learned mental models for what different colors mean:
- Blue = trust, professionalism, breadth (think Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn)
- Red = urgency, excitement, intensity (think YouTube, Netflix, gambling)
- Green = growth, money, nature (think Spotify, money apps)
- Pink = playfulness, narrowness, casualness (think beauty apps, dating apps)

If your product doesn't match the mental model, you create cognitive dissonance. Users will be confused or skeptical.

**3. Consider the cross-platform experience.**

Pink needs to work:
- On iOS (light and dark mode)
- On Android
- On desktop web
- In marketing materials
- In social media
- In customer support contexts

Does pink signal "comprehensive unified platform" in all those contexts? Or does it signal "playful mobile game"?

Test the full ecosystem, not just the icon in isolation.

**4. Audit for accessibility.**

While not directly related to pink, any color choice needs accessibility consideration:
- Contrast ratios (WCAG compliance)
- Color blindness accommodations
- Cultural color associations (pink has different meanings in different markets)

Make sure pink doesn't create accessibility barriers.

**Final UX Recommendation:**

Pink is a high-risk choice for a unified platform. It creates expectation mismatches, doesn't signal comprehensiveness, and may alienate key user segments.

If Yes Games proceeds with pink, they need to:
- Conduct extensive user testing across all verticals
- Ensure the in-app experience matches the playful promise of pink
- Be prepared for user confusion about what the platform actually offers
- Invest heavily in onboarding to explain the comprehensive nature

Alternatively, choose a color that naturally signals breadth, trust, and professionalism. That would reduce UX friction and set accurate expectations from the first interaction.

Pink might work for a focused product. For a unified platform, it's a UX mismatch.
