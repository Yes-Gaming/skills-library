# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Pink Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Nikolaj (Variation 2.5 - Analytical/Comparative Style)
**Age:** 34
**Location:** Copenhagen, Østerbro
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Pink app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a pink app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

My first reaction is to question the strategic positioning. Pink is a deliberate departure from category norms, which suggests either genuine innovation or misguided differentiation. As someone who evaluates betting platforms analytically, I'd want to understand the reasoning before forming an opinion.

---

**Q2: How does pink make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Pink creates a specific perceptual profile that I can map against the key attributes I value in a betting platform. Let me break this down:

**What I need from a betting platform:**
1. Speed and reliability (critical for live betting)
2. Data depth and accuracy (for informed decisions)
3. Professional interface (for serious analysis)
4. Trustworthiness and security (for financial transactions)
5. Competitive odds and market depth (for value)

**What pink signals:**
1. Approachability and friendliness (high)
2. Playfulness and casualness (high)
3. Modernity and trendiness (moderate to high)
4. Professionalism and seriousness (low)
5. Trust and security (low to neutral)

There's a significant mismatch here. Pink scores well on approachability, which is fine, but it scores poorly on professionalism, seriousness, and trust—all of which are critical for someone like me who treats betting as a calculated financial activity.

---

**Q3: Would pink help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, from a pure recognition efficiency standpoint, pink would perform well. High color differentiation correlates with faster visual search times. That's a functional advantage.

However, recognition speed is only one variable in my decision-making matrix. I also evaluate:
- Does the app signal competence? (Pink: low)
- Does it signal data-driven analysis? (Pink: low)
- Does it signal trustworthiness? (Pink: low)

So while I'd find the app quickly, I'd also be less confident that it's the right platform for my needs. That's a net negative trade-off.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a pink app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

**2 out of 5.**

Here's my analytical breakdown:

**Pros:**
- High differentiation (+0.5)
- Excellent recognition speed (+0.5)
- Modern positioning (+0.5)

**Cons:**
- Low professionalism signal (-1.0)
- Low trust signal (-1.0)
- Poor fit for analytical/strategic betting (-0.5)

Net score: 2/5.

Pink would work if Yes Games is targeting casual, fun-oriented users who don't take betting seriously. But if they're targeting analytical, data-driven users like me—people who study odds, track performance, and make calculated wagers—pink is a strategic mismatch.

I'd need to see data showing that Yes Games' core demographic values approachability over professionalism before I'd endorse this choice. Without that data, pink feels like a risky bet (pun intended).

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about pink as our brand color for the app icon?**

A few strategic considerations:

**1. Demographic alignment is critical.**
If Yes Games' target users are 18-25 casual bettors who value fun and approachability, pink could work. But if the target is 30-45 analytical bettors who value data and professionalism, pink will underperform.

I'd recommend segmenting your user base and testing pink specifically with each segment. Measure:
- Trust perception
- Competence perception
- Likelihood to download
- Likelihood to bet significant amounts

My hypothesis: pink will score well with younger, casual users and poorly with older, serious users.

**2. Competitive positioning matters.**
If every competitor uses dark blue or black, pink creates clear differentiation. But differentiation only matters if it attracts the right users. If you differentiate yourself right out of your target market, that's a strategic error.

Analyze your competitors' positioning:
- Are they all targeting serious, high-value bettors? If yes, pink could capture the underserved casual segment.
- Are they ignoring younger, fun-oriented users? If yes, pink makes strategic sense.
- Are serious bettors your highest lifetime value segment? If yes, pink is a mistake.

**3. Execution quality is non-negotiable.**
If you proceed with pink, it must be executed flawlessly. The shade, typography, and overall design need to feel premium and modern, not cheap or gimmicky. A poorly executed pink icon will be worse than a well-executed blue icon.

**Final recommendation:**
From my perspective as a 34-year-old financial analyst who bets systematically, pink doesn't work. It signals casualness and fun when I need professionalism and trust.

But I recognize I might not be the target demographic. If Yes Games is going after a different user profile, pink could be strategically sound. The key is knowing exactly who you're targeting and ensuring pink aligns with their values.

My advice: test it rigorously before committing. Don't guess. Measure.
