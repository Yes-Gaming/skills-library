# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Pink Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Peter (Variation 2.2 - Detailed/Explanatory Style)
**Age:** 32
**Location:** Aarhus, Brabrand
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Pink app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a pink app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

My first reaction is surprise, honestly. Pink is not a color I associate with sports betting or strategic gambling. When I think about the apps I use for betting—analyzing team stats, studying odds movements, placing calculated wagers—pink doesn't fit that mental model.

Pink reads as playful, approachable, maybe even whimsical. But sports betting, especially the way I approach it, is analytical and strategic. It's about reading data, understanding probability, making informed decisions under time pressure. Pink feels like it's signaling something different—something lighter, less serious. That creates a disconnect for me.

---

**Q2: How does pink make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Pink makes me feel like the app is positioning itself as casual entertainment rather than a serious betting platform. And that's fine if that's the intention, but it's not what I'm looking for.

When I'm betting on a Superliga match, I'm not looking for "fun and playful." I'm looking for speed, depth, reliability, and data. I need live odds that update instantly. I need comprehensive statistics. I need a platform that feels like a tool, not a toy.

Pink signals toy. It signals leisure. It doesn't signal the competitive edge I want when I'm putting real money on the line based on analysis I've spent hours doing. If I saw a pink betting app, I'd assume it's targeting people who bet for fun, not people who bet strategically.

---

**Q3: Would pink help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, pink would be highly visible. From a pure recognition standpoint, it works. I don't have any pink apps, so it would stand out immediately.

But here's the thing: recognition speed isn't my only concern. I also care about what the app signals. If I can find the app quickly but it doesn't feel like the right tool for the job, that's a problem. Pink might help me find it faster, but it doesn't make me feel confident that it's the best platform for serious sports betting.

---

**Q4: Does pink give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

Pink gives me:
- **Playfulness** (strong association)
- **Friendliness and approachability** (very strong)
- **Casualness** (moderate to strong)
- **Softness** (literally and metaphorically)

It does NOT give me:
- **Competitive intensity** (which I want for sports betting)
- **Analytical rigor** (which I need for strategic betting)
- **Professionalism or trust** (which are baseline requirements)

If I compare pink to other colors:
- **Blue** signals trust, data, professionalism (ideal for analytics-driven betting)
- **Red** signals intensity, urgency, competition (good for live betting energy)
- **Dark green or teal** signals money, growth, strategic thinking (works for betting)
- **Pink** signals fun, approachability, lightness (misaligned with strategic sports betting)

So from my perspective as someone who treats betting as a calculated activity, pink feels off-brand.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a pink app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

**2.5 out of 5.**

I'm giving it a 2.5 because I can see the logic—differentiation, visibility, modern positioning. But it doesn't resonate with me as a strategic sports bettor.

If Yes Games is positioning itself as a casual, fun betting platform for people who don't take it too seriously, pink could work. But if it's positioning itself as a platform for people who do deep research, analyze statistics, and make informed wagers, pink undermines that positioning.

I'd want to know: who is the target user? If it's me—a 32-year-old software developer who studies team stats and odds movements—pink is the wrong choice. If it's someone who bets casually for fun without much analysis, pink might be fine.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about pink as our brand color for the app icon?**

Yeah, I'd encourage Yes Games to think carefully about what they want to be known for. If you want to be the fun, approachable, casual betting app, pink supports that. But if you want to be the platform serious bettors choose because of data depth, analytical tools, and strategic features, pink contradicts that.

Color is a signal. It tells users what to expect before they even open the app. Pink signals "light and fun." If the app inside is actually robust, data-rich, and serious, there's going to be a mismatch. Users like me will hesitate to trust it because the aesthetic doesn't match the function.

My advice: test pink specifically with your core demographic. If your core users are casual bettors in their 20s who value fun and approachability, pink might perform well. But if your core users are analytical, data-driven bettors in their 30s and 40s, pink is going to be a turn-off.

Personally, I'd prefer a color that signals competence, depth, and seriousness—something like a deep blue, dark teal, or even a sophisticated gray. Those colors tell me: "This platform is for people who know what they're doing." Pink tells me: "This platform is for people who are just messing around."

And I'm not just messing around when I bet.
