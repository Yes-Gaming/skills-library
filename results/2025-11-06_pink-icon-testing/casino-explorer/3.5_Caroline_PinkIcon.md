# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Pink Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Caroline (Variation 3.5 - Analytical/Comparative Style)
**Age:** 29
**Location:** Copenhagen, Østerbro
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Pink app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a pink app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

My first reaction is to analyze the strategic logic. Pink is statistically uncommon in the gambling app category, which creates differentiation. But differentiation without strategic alignment is just noise. So I'd want to understand: what user need does pink serve? What brand attributes is it communicating?

---

**Q2: How does pink make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Pink in gaming is mathematically interesting because it occupies a unique position in the color-emotion matrix. Let me map it out:

**Traditional casino colors and their psychological profiles:**
- **Red:** High arousal, urgency, excitement (dopamine trigger)
- **Gold/Yellow:** Wealth association, optimism, attention
- **Black:** Luxury, exclusivity, seriousness
- **Blue:** Trust, security, professionalism
- **Purple:** Mystery, royalty, premium

**Pink's psychological profile:**
- **Arousal level:** Moderate to low (calming effect)
- **Emotional tone:** Positive, friendly, approachable
- **Cultural associations:** Playfulness, youthfulness, gender-coding (diminishing but present)
- **Trust signal:** Neutral to low

From a mathematical RTP and gaming perspective, I care about whether the color signals honesty and transparency. Pink doesn't inherently signal those attributes. Blue does. Gray does. Pink signals friendliness, which is nice but not my primary concern when I'm evaluating RTP percentages and house edge.

---

**Q3: Would pink help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, objectively. Pink would have high visual salience due to its rarity in my app ecosystem. From a cognitive load perspective, unique colors reduce search time by approximately 30-40% compared to common colors like blue or green.

That's a functional benefit. But function doesn't equal value. If pink helps me find an app I don't trust or don't enjoy, that's not useful.

---

**Q4: Does pink give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

Let me score pink across key attributes on a 1-10 scale, based on color psychology research and my own analytical framework:

**Pink scores:**
- **Fun:** 8/10 (high association)
- **Approachability:** 9/10 (very high)
- **Playfulness:** 8/10 (high)
- **Modernity:** 7/10 (context-dependent)
- **Transparency:** 5/10 (neutral, doesn't signal honesty or dishonesty)
- **Trust:** 4/10 (low to neutral)
- **Professionalism:** 3/10 (low)
- **RTP transparency:** 2/10 (no correlation)

**What I need from a casino app:**
- **RTP transparency:** 10/10 priority
- **Game fairness:** 10/10 priority
- **Mathematical clarity:** 9/10 priority
- **Trustworthy licensing:** 9/10 priority
- **Professional interface:** 7/10 priority

Pink scores well on approachability (which I don't prioritize) and poorly on trust and professionalism (which I do prioritize). That's a mismatch.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a pink app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

**3 out of 5.**

Here's my analytical breakdown:

**Pros (+1.5 points):**
- High differentiation and recognition speed (functional advantage)
- Modern positioning (appeals to younger demographics)
- Approachable tone (reduces intimidation factor)

**Cons (-1.5 points):**
- Low trust signaling (critical for gambling apps)
- Poor alignment with analytical/mathematical users (my profile)
- Risk of appearing unprofessional or unserious (perception risk)

I'm giving it a 3 because I can see the strategic logic for certain demographics, but it doesn't resonate with my priorities as someone who evaluates casinos based on mathematics and transparency.

If Yes Games is targeting casual, fun-oriented players who don't analyze RTP, pink might work. But if they're targeting players like me—people who compare RTP percentages, study volatility metrics, and choose games based on mathematical advantage—pink sends the wrong signal.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about pink as our brand color for the app icon?**

A few analytical considerations:

**1. Demographic segmentation is critical.**
Pink will perform differently across user segments. My hypothesis:
- **18-25, casual players:** Pink scores 4-5/5 (high appeal)
- **25-35, analytical players:** Pink scores 2-3/5 (low to neutral appeal)
- **35+, traditional casino players:** Pink scores 1-2/5 (low appeal)

If Yes Games' core demographic is the first group, pink is strategically sound. If it's the second or third group, pink is a mistake.

I'd recommend running quantitative tests:
- Survey 500+ users across demographics
- Measure trust perception, download intent, and brand attribute association
- Segment results by age, gender, betting style, and frequency

Don't guess. Measure.

**2. Pink doesn't solve the trust problem.**
As someone who cares deeply about RTP transparency and game fairness, pink doesn't make me trust Yes Games more. If anything, it makes me slightly more skeptical because it feels like aesthetic differentiation rather than operational differentiation.

What would make me trust Yes Games?
- Publicly posted RTP percentages for every game
- Third-party audit reports (eCOGRA, iTech Labs)
- Clear responsible gambling tools (loss limits, self-exclusion)
- Transparent terms and conditions

If Yes Games does all of that AND uses pink, I'd be fine with pink. But if pink is the primary differentiator without operational substance, it's just lipstick on a slot machine.

**3. Execution quality is non-negotiable.**
If you proceed with pink, it must be executed flawlessly:
- **Shade selection:** Dusty rose or salmon (sophisticated) > hot pink (juvenile)
- **Typography:** Bold, modern sans-serif (confident) > script or thin fonts (weak)
- **Finish:** Subtle gradient or texture (premium) > flat color (basic)

Poor execution will make pink feel cheap. Excellent execution could make it feel premium.

**4. Test against alternatives.**
Don't just test pink in isolation. Test it against:
- Dark blue (trust, professionalism)
- Teal (modern, data-driven)
- Deep purple (premium, mysterious)
- Charcoal with pink accent (balanced approach)

Measure which option best communicates the brand attributes Yes Games wants to own.

**Final recommendation:**
Pink is a high-risk choice for mathematically-minded, RTP-conscious players like me. It doesn't signal the transparency and professionalism I value. But it might work for younger, casual players who prioritize fun over analysis.

Know your target demographic. Design for them, not for everyone. And back up whatever color you choose with operational excellence. Color is just the wrapper—what's inside matters more.
