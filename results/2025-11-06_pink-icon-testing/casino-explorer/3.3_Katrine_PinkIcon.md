# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Pink Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Katrine (Variation 3.3 - Skeptical/Critical Style)
**Age:** 24
**Location:** Copenhagen, Nørrebro
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Pink app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a pink app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

My first thought is: "Why?" As a psychology student, I'm immediately curious about the strategic reasoning here. Pink is such a loaded color—it has cultural baggage, gender associations, and psychological effects that are well-documented. So I want to know: is Yes Games using pink intentionally to leverage those associations, or is this just aesthetic trend-chasing?

---

**Q2: How does pink make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Pink in gambling makes me suspicious because it feels like emotional manipulation. Here's why:

Pink is psychologically calming. It reduces arousal and aggression. That's why it's used in hospitals, prisons, and therapy spaces. But gambling—especially slot machines and casino games—are designed to be high-arousal activities. They're engineered to trigger dopamine, create excitement, and keep you engaged.

So if a gambling app uses pink, what's the intention? Are they trying to:
1. Make gambling feel less risky than it actually is? (Concerning)
2. Calm users so they stay longer and bet more? (Manipulative)
3. Appeal to demographics who might otherwise avoid gambling? (Predatory)

I'm not saying Yes Games is doing any of this intentionally, but as someone studying behavioral psychology, these are the questions pink raises for me. Color isn't neutral—it's a psychological tool. And in gambling, where design is already weaponized to maximize engagement and spending, I'm skeptical of any color choice that feels calculated to disarm users.

---

**Q3: Would pink help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, pink would be highly visible. But that doesn't address my concern. Visibility isn't the same as trustworthiness. Just because I can find the app doesn't mean I should use it.

In fact, high visibility combined with psychological manipulation is worse than low visibility with honesty. If pink is being used to draw me in while softening my perception of risk, that's a problem.

---

**Q4: Does pink give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

From a psychological perspective, here's what pink signals:

**Documented associations:**
- **Calmness and reduced aggression** (Baker-Miller Pink effect, well-studied)
- **Femininity and gentleness** (cultural conditioning, though changing)
- **Playfulness and youthfulness** (common in toys, children's products)
- **Approachability and non-threat** (used in branding to appear friendly)

**What pink does NOT signal:**
- **Seriousness or professionalism** (pink is not a "serious" color)
- **Trust or security** (blue and gray are trust colors, not pink)
- **High stakes or intensity** (pink is low-arousal)

**In a gambling context, here's my concern:**
Gambling involves real money, addiction risk, and psychological vulnerability. Using a calming, friendly, playful color like pink could:
- Minimize users' perception of risk ("it's just fun, it's harmless")
- Lower psychological barriers to spending ("it doesn't feel serious, so it's okay")
- Exploit the approachability association to attract vulnerable users

I'm not accusing Yes Games of being predatory, but these are the psychological mechanisms at play whether they intend them or not. Color has power, and pink's power is to soften, calm, and disarm. In gambling, that's ethically questionable.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a pink app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

**2 out of 5.**

I'm giving it a 2 because I don't think pink is inherently evil, but I'm deeply skeptical of its use in gambling. If Yes Games can demonstrate that:
1. Pink is not being used to manipulate risk perception
2. The app includes industry-leading responsible gambling tools
3. They're transparent about odds, house edge, and addiction risks
4. They're genuinely trying to make gambling more approachable for the right reasons (reducing stigma, not exploiting vulnerability)

...then I might bump it to a 3. But without that evidence, pink feels like a red flag to me. It's a color choice that softens the perception of an activity that should be taken seriously.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about pink as our brand color for the app icon?**

Here's my psychology student take: color is never neutral, especially in contexts like gambling where design is already heavily optimized to influence behavior.

If Yes Games chooses pink, they need to ask themselves:
- **Why pink?** Is it because they genuinely want to be more approachable and reduce the intimidation factor of gambling? Or is it because they think pink will attract more users and increase engagement?
- **Who benefits from pink?** Does pink help users make better, more informed decisions? Or does it help the company by making gambling feel safer than it is?
- **What's the full picture?** Is pink part of a broader strategy to be more ethical and transparent? Or is it just aesthetic branding on top of the same exploitative mechanics every other gambling app uses?

If the answers are user-centric and ethical, pink could be defensible. But if the answers are company-centric and revenue-driven, pink is just another manipulation tactic.

My advice: Only use pink if you're willing to back it up with genuine, substantive changes to how you design and operate your platform. Otherwise, you're just using color psychology to exploit users more effectively, and that's ethically indefensible.

As someone studying this stuff, I'll be watching.
