# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Pink Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Marie (Variation 1.5 - Analytical/Comparative Style)
**Age:** 26
**Location:** Copenhagen, Østerbro
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Pink app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a pink app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

My first reaction is analytical curiosity. Pink is statistically rare in the gambling app category, which means it would create high differentiation. From a recognition standpoint, that's valuable. But I'd immediately want to understand the strategic reasoning—why pink specifically? What brand attributes is it trying to communicate?

---

**Q2: How does pink make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Pink in gaming creates an interesting psychological profile. Looking at the data, pink typically signals:
- Approachability (70-80% association rate in color psychology studies)
- Playfulness (similar range)
- Modernity (when used in tech/digital contexts)

Compared to traditional gambling colors:
- **Red** signals excitement and urgency (high arousal)
- **Blue** signals trust and security (low arousal, high trust)
- **Black/Gold** signals premium and exclusivity
- **Pink** signals friendliness and accessibility (moderate arousal, moderate trust)

So pink positions Yes Games in a unique quadrant—less intense than red, less formal than blue, more approachable than black. That could be strategically smart if your target demographic values accessibility over intimidation.

---

**Q3: Would pink help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, objectively. Pink would have extremely high salience on a typical smartphone home screen. Looking at my own app distribution:
- Blue apps: ~30%
- Multi-color apps: ~25%
- Green apps: ~15%
- Red apps: ~10%
- Gray/Black apps: ~10%
- Yellow apps: ~5%
- Pink apps: ~0%

So pink would be a statistical outlier, which correlates with faster visual search times. Research suggests unique colors can reduce app location time by 30-50%. From a pure functionality standpoint, pink scores very high.

---

**Q4: Does pink give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

Let me break down the association profile:

**Positive Associations:**
- Fun: 8/10 (strong)
- Friendly: 9/10 (very strong)
- Approachable: 9/10 (very strong)
- Modern: 7/10 (moderate-strong)

**Neutral/Context-Dependent:**
- Energy: 5/10 (moderate, but softer energy than red or yellow)
- Premium: 4/10 (depends heavily on shade and execution)
- Trust: 5/10 (neutral—pink doesn't strongly signal trust or distrust)

**Potential Negative Associations:**
- Feminine coding: 6/10 risk (cultural baggage, though evolving)
- Perceived as "cute" rather than "serious": 5/10 risk
- May not signal security/legitimacy: 6/10 concern

**Compared to competitors:**
If I'm analyzing this against other gambling platforms, pink would differentiate but potentially sacrifice some trust signals. The question is whether the differentiation benefit outweighs the trust cost. That's a strategic trade-off that depends on your target demographic and positioning.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a pink app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

**3.5 out of 5.**

Here's my reasoning:

**Pros (+1.5 points):**
- High differentiation (data-driven advantage)
- Excellent recognition speed (functional benefit)
- Modern and approachable positioning (strategic clarity)

**Cons (-1.5 points):**
- Uncertain trust impact (risk factor)
- Potential demographic mismatch for older/conservative users (market risk)
- Requires excellent execution to avoid "gimmicky" perception (execution dependency)

I'd need more data to move from 3.5 to 4 or higher:
1. **Target demographic analysis:** What's the age and psychographic breakdown of your core users? If you're targeting 18-30 casual gamers, pink scores higher. If you're targeting 45+ serious bettors, it scores lower.

2. **Brand attribute prioritization:** Is "approachable and fun" more important than "trustworthy and serious"? Your answer determines whether pink is strategically aligned.

3. **Competitive positioning:** If every competitor uses dark blue/black, pink creates a clear alternative. But you'd need to ensure there's demand for that alternative positioning.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about pink as our brand color for the app icon?**

A few analytical considerations:

**1. Shade specificity matters enormously.**
- Hot pink (e.g., #FF69B4): Youthful, trendy, potentially perceived as feminine
- Dusty rose (e.g., #DCAE96): More premium, sophisticated, gender-neutral
- Salmon pink (e.g., #FA8072): Energetic but softer than red, broadly appealing

The exact hex code will significantly impact perception. I'd recommend A/B testing multiple shades.

**2. Test with your actual user base.**
Synthetic personas are useful for hypothesis generation, but you'd need real-world data to validate. Run a preference test with 200-500 users across your target demographics. Measure:
- Recognition speed
- Brand attribute association
- Trust perception
- Download intent

**3. Consider the full ecosystem.**
The icon doesn't exist in isolation. If you go pink:
- What does the splash screen look like?
- What's the primary color palette inside the app?
- How does pink translate to marketing materials?
- Does it work across all touchpoints (social media, ads, website)?

Pink needs to be part of a cohesive design system, not just an icon choice.

**4. Benchmark against analogous brands.**
Look at brands that successfully use pink in trust-dependent categories:
- T-Mobile (telecommunications—trust-dependent, uses magenta successfully)
- Lyft (ride-sharing—safety-dependent, uses pink effectively)
- Klarna (fintech—financial trust, uses pink confidently)

These brands prove pink CAN work in categories where trust matters, but they back it up with operational excellence. The color isn't doing the trust-building; the company is. Pink is just the aesthetic wrapper.

**Final recommendation:**
Pink is a strategically interesting choice with high risk and high reward potential. I'd give it a qualified "yes" if:
- Your target demographic skews younger (18-35)
- You're prioritizing approachability over traditional trust signals
- You're willing to invest in exceptional execution
- You have strong operational trust-building mechanisms (licensing, support, responsible gambling tools)

But I'd advise against it if:
- Your core users are 40+ and value traditional professionalism
- You're in a highly competitive market where trust is the primary differentiator
- You're not confident in flawless design execution

It's a calculated risk. Make sure you're calculating it correctly.
