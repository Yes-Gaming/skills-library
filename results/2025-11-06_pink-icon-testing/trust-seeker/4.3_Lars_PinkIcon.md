# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Pink Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Lars (Variation 4.3 - Skeptical/Critical Style)
**Age:** 44
**Location:** Odense, Seden
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Pink app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a pink app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

My first reaction, as an IT security consultant, is immediate skepticism. Pink is such an unconventional choice for a gambling platform that it raises red flags. In my experience auditing digital platforms, companies that prioritize aesthetic differentiation over clear trust signals are often trying to distract from something—either poor security practices, weak regulatory compliance, or predatory business models.

I'm not saying Yes Games is doing any of that, but pink makes me want to audit them more carefully before trusting them with my money.

---

**Q2: How does pink make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Pink makes me deeply uncomfortable in a gambling context because it feels like it's designed to lower my defenses. Let me explain from a security and psychology perspective:

**Security red flags:**
When I evaluate whether to use a platform, I look for clear trust signals:
- HTTPS and proper certificate management
- Visible regulatory licensing (Spillemyndigheden badge)
- Professional visual design that signals operational maturity
- Clear privacy policy and data handling practices
- Two-factor authentication and strong security features

Pink doesn't signal any of those things. Pink signals "we're friendly and fun," which is not what I need from a platform that will store my payment information and personal data.

**Psychological manipulation concerns:**
Pink is a calming color. It's used in therapeutic contexts specifically to reduce anxiety and aggression. If a gambling platform uses pink, I have to ask: why are they trying to calm me down?

Gambling should make users slightly alert and cautious—it's a financial activity with real risks. Using a calming color to lower that natural caution feels manipulative. It's like a dark pattern in visual form.

---

**Q3: Would pink help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, technically. But that's like saying a bright red "FREE MONEY" banner would get my attention. Visibility isn't the same as trustworthiness.

In fact, if I saw a pink gambling app on my phone, I'd probably want to audit how it got there. Did I download it intentionally? Is it legitimate? Pink makes me question legitimacy, not celebrate visibility.

---

**Q4: Does pink give you any specific associations or feelings (energy, fun, caution, cheap, premium, trust, etc.)?**

From a security professional's perspective, here's what pink signals:

**Negative associations:**
- **Lack of seriousness** (gambling involves real money and should be taken seriously)
- **Potential manipulation** (using calming colors to reduce caution)
- **Weak security culture** (companies serious about security don't play with branding gimmicks)
- **Possible regulatory non-compliance** (legitimate operators signal compliance, not trendiness)
- **Amateur operation** (pink feels like a startup trying to be disruptive without substance)

**What pink does NOT signal:**
- **Trust** (absolutely not—trust requires traditional, proven signals)
- **Security** (pink doesn't say "we protect your data and money")
- **Professionalism** (pink is the opposite of professional in this context)
- **Regulatory compliance** (licensed operators use serious branding)
- **Technical competence** (pink doesn't inspire confidence in their technical infrastructure)

When I see pink in gambling, I think: "This is a company that prioritized marketing aesthetics over operational trust-building." That's a massive red flag.

---

**Q5: On a scale of 1-5, how much do you like the idea of a pink app icon? (1=hate it, 5=love it)**

**1 out of 5.**

I'm giving it a 1 because, from a security and trust perspective, pink actively undermines confidence. It's not just neutral or unhelpful—it's actively harmful to trust-building.

Here's why:

**Security concerns:**
- Companies serious about security use serious branding
- Pink signals that aesthetics > substance
- I would not trust a pink gambling app with my payment information without extensive vetting

**Trust deficit:**
- Pink doesn't signal licensing or regulatory compliance
- Pink doesn't signal professional operations
- Pink doesn't signal financial stability or business maturity

**Professional assessment:**
If a client asked me to security-audit a pink gambling app, my initial skepticism would be much higher than for a blue or gray app. I'd assume they're cutting corners somewhere, and I'd audit more carefully.

That's not fair to Yes Games if they're actually doing everything right, but perception matters. And pink creates a negative perception from a trust and security standpoint.

---

**Q6: Any other thoughts about pink as our brand color for the app icon?**

Yeah, here's my IT security consultant take:

**1. Trust signals matter more than aesthetic differentiation.**
In gambling—and in any financial or data-sensitive service—users need clear, immediate trust signals. Those signals are cultural and learned:
- Blue = trust, security, professionalism (decades of conditioning)
- Gray/Black = serious, professional, established
- Green (specific shade) = money, banking, financial

Pink has no established trust association in gambling or finance. You'd be starting from scratch, trying to build trust with a color that actively works against you.

**2. Pink suggests weak security culture.**
When I assess a company's security posture, I look at everything—including branding. Companies with strong security cultures tend to be serious, professional, and traditional in their presentation. They know trust is fragile and don't take unnecessary risks with it.

Companies that prioritize "disruption" or "being different" in their branding often have weaker security cultures. They're optimizing for growth and virality, not for safety and trust.

Pink signals the latter to me. It says: "We care more about standing out than building trust." That's concerning.

**3. You're inviting extra scrutiny.**
If Yes Games launches with pink branding, people like me—security professionals, regulators, journalists, skeptical users—will scrutinize you more carefully. You'll be under a microscope precisely because you're breaking norms.

That's fine if your operations are flawless. But if there are any security gaps, compliance issues, or user experience problems, they'll be amplified by the unconventional branding. Pink sets higher expectations for perfection because it signals confidence. If you can't deliver on that confidence, it'll backfire.

**4. Consider your threat model.**
Gambling platforms are high-value targets for fraud, hacking, and regulatory action. You need users to trust you enough to provide sensitive data and payment information. You also need regulators to see you as a serious, compliant operator.

Pink undermines both. It makes users question your legitimacy and makes regulators wonder if you're a serious business or a flash-in-the-pan startup.

**Final recommendation:**
From a security and trust perspective, pink is a terrible choice for a gambling app. It signals everything you don't want to signal: lack of seriousness, potential manipulation, weak professional culture, and uncertain legitimacy.

If Yes Games wants to build trust—especially with users like me who are cautious and security-conscious—choose a color that signals trust. Blue, gray, dark green. Not pink.

If you proceed with pink, you'll need to work 10x harder to prove your legitimacy and trustworthiness. Is that a fight worth having? I don't think so.
