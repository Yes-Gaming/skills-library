# User Research Interview: App Icon Design Concept
## Yes Games - Pink Background Icon Testing

**Persona:** Søren (Variation 4.5 - Analytical/Comparative Style)
**Age:** 51
**Location:** Copenhagen, Hellerup
**Date:** November 6, 2025
**Concept Tested:** Pink app icon with YES logotype

---

## Interview Questions & Responses

**Q1: What's your first reaction when you see a pink app icon on your iPhone home screen?**

My first reaction is analytical curiosity mixed with professional skepticism. As a financial advisor, I evaluate trust signals across all financial services—including gambling platforms, which I consider a form of entertainment spending that requires similar due diligence to other financial decisions.

Pink is such a departure from category norms that it signals either genuine strategic innovation or superficial differentiation without substance. I'd need to investigate further before forming a definitive opinion, but my initial bias is skeptical.

---

**Q2: How does pink make you feel in the context of a gaming/entertainment app?**

Pink creates a significant trust deficit for me, and I'll explain why using a framework I apply to all financial services:

**Trust Evaluation Framework for Gambling Platforms:**

1. **Regulatory Compliance Signals** (Weight: 30%)
   - Pink score: 2/10 (does not signal regulatory seriousness)
   - Blue/Gray score: 9/10 (traditional trust colors)

2. **Financial Stability Indicators** (Weight: 25%)
   - Pink score: 3/10 (doesn't signal established business)
   - Dark colors score: 8/10 (signal longevity and stability)

3. **Professionalism and Competence** (Weight: 25%)
   - Pink score: 2/10 (signals playfulness, not competence)
   - Traditional colors score: 9/10 (signal professionalism)

4. **Transparency and Honesty** (Weight: 20%)
   - Pink score: 5/10 (neutral—doesn't signal transparency either way)
   - Clear design score: 8/10 (any color, if executed professionally)

**Pink's composite trust score: 2.9/10**

That's significantly below the threshold where I'd consider using a platform without extensive additional research. Pink creates immediate trust concerns that would need to be overcome through exceptional operational evidence.

---

**Q3: Would pink help you find and recognize the app quickly among your other apps?**

Yes, from a pure usability standpoint, pink would have high visual salience. But as I tell my clients: convenience is never an excuse to bypass due diligence.

Fast access to a potentially untrustworthy platform is worse than slow access to a trustworthy one. Pink optimizes for the wrong variable.

**Q6: Any other thoughts about pink as our brand color for the app icon?**

Several strategic considerations from a financial advisory perspective:

**1. Lifetime Value Economics**

In gambling, the highest lifetime value (LTV) users are typically:
- Older demographics (40+, like me)
- Higher income (50,000+ DKK/month)
- Longer engagement duration (years, not months)
- Responsible gambling behavior (consistent, budgeted spending)

Pink appeals least to this demographic. We value trust, professionalism, and regulatory compliance signals. Pink provides none of those.

If Yes Games optimizes for short-term user acquisition of younger, lower-LTV users, pink might work. But if you're optimizing for long-term, high-LTV users, pink is counterproductive.

**2. Regulatory Perception Management**

Gambling platforms operate in a heavily regulated environment. Regulators (Spillemyndigheden in Denmark) evaluate operators on professionalism, compliance, and responsible gambling practices.

While color doesn't directly impact regulatory compliance, it does impact perception. Pink signals "trendy and casual" rather than "serious and compliant." If regulators are evaluating two operators—one with professional blue branding and one with pink—which do they perceive as more serious about compliance?

Perception matters in regulated industries. Pink creates unnecessary perception risk.

**3. Institutional Trust Signals**

When I evaluate whether to recommend a gambling platform to clients (as entertainment spending, not investment), I look for institutional trust signals:
- Established brand (years in operation)
- Professional visual identity
- Clear regulatory licensing
- Transparent financial practices
- Positive industry reputation

Pink undermines the "professional visual identity" criterion. It makes the platform seem less established, less serious, less trustworthy—even if operationally they're excellent.

**4. Competitive Positioning Analysis**

I analyzed the color strategies of top-performing gambling operators:
- **Bet365:** Blue (trust, professionalism)
- **888:** Dark with gold accents (premium, established)
- **Unibet:** Green (stability, growth)
- **PokerStars:** Red with professional design (energy with credibility)

None use pink. That's either:
- An opportunity (blue ocean positioning)
- A signal (category learnings suggest pink doesn't work)

My hypothesis: it's the latter. Established operators avoid pink because high-LTV users don't respond to it positively. They've tested it and moved on.

**Final Recommendation:**

From a financial advisory perspective, I would not recommend pink for a gambling platform targeting users like me. The trust deficit is too significant, and the competitive positioning is misaligned with high-value user preferences.

**Alternative Strategies:**

1. **Professional Blue with Pink Accents:** Maintains trust while adding differentiation
2. **Sophisticated Dusty Rose with Dark Typography:** Balances uniqueness with professionalism
3. **Dark Background with Pink Highlights:** Modern differentiation without sacrificing credibility

But pure pink as primary brand color? That's a high-risk choice that alienates your most valuable users. I'd advise against it unless Yes Games is explicitly targeting a different demographic than me—and willing to accept lower lifetime value as a trade-off for differentiation.
