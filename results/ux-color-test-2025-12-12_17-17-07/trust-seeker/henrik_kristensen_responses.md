# UX Color Variation Testing - Henrik Kristensen (Trust Seeker)
**Tester:** Henrik Kristensen, 47, Senior Consultant, Copenhagen, Denmark
**Testing Date:** 2025-12-12
**Testing Approach:** Conservative, trust-focused evaluation with emphasis on control perception and professional legitimacy

---

## VARIATION 1 - Dark Blue/Navy with Purple Accents

### Q1: Overall Trust Assessment
**Rating:** 7/10
I see a professional foundation here. The dark navy evokes stability and banking/financial security - good heritage. However, the purple accent is concerning. It feels slightly trendy and forced, like someone wanted to add "personality" without understanding the context. This undermines trust slightly.

### Q2: Perceived Safety and Security
**Rating:** 6/10
Dark blue typically signals security. But the purple distraction creates ambiguity. When I'm placing bets, I need absolute clarity about who I'm trusting. This design whispers rather than assures. The navy works; the purple contradicts it.

### Q3: Visual Clarity and Readability
**Rating:** 8/10
Good contrast. I can read everything without strain. The font hierarchy is clear. This is done right - no tricks, no hidden text, proper spacing. Respectable professional work.

### Q4: Control Perception ("I'm in charge")
**Rating:** 6/10
The interface appears to guide me, which is fine, but the purple accents create visual pressure points that feel designed to direct my attention. I notice this manipulation attempt. Not subtle enough. I want to feel in control, not directed.

### Q5: Design Legitimacy (Established Feel)
**Rating:** 7/10
Mostly legitimate. Conservative color palette with established financial institution heritage. But the purple accent screams "modern redesign" - which sometimes means cutting corners elsewhere. This makes me slightly suspicious.

### Q6: Pressure Detection (Visual/Psychological)
**Rating:** 4/10
MODERATE PRESSURE DETECTED. The purple is strategic. It's drawing attention to specific actions. I can see the deliberate color choice to emphasize call-to-action buttons. This is manipulative. I resist designs that push me.

### Q7: Professional Appearance
**Rating:** 8/10
Competent design. Clean layout. Proper use of whitespace. But the purple accent prevents this from being "premium" - it's "respectable with a young designer's touch."

### Q8: Transparency/Openness Feeling
**Rating:** 7/10
Decent. The dark blue doesn't hide anything. Text is legible. But purple accents create visual opacity - they emphasize certain elements over others, which feels like selective disclosure.

### Q9: Text Hierarchy and Information Structure
**Rating:** 8/10
Well-organized. I can understand the priority of information. No confusion about what matters. Good work technically.

### Q10: Skepticism Level (Am I doubting?)
**Rating:** 5/10
MODERATE SKEPTICISM. The purple choice feels like a conversion optimization decision. It's chosen specifically to influence behavior. I ask myself: "Why this color?" That question creates doubt.

### Q11: Accessibility and Cognitive Load
**Rating:** 8/10
Easy to process. No excessive animations. No clutter. A conservative person can navigate this without feeling overwhelmed. Good consideration for older users (like myself).

### Q12: Would You Use This Service Long-Term?
**Rating:** 6/10
Possibly, but with reservations. The purple accent suggests the company is more focused on conversion optimization than on my long-term security. I'd need to verify their credentials elsewhere before committing real money.
